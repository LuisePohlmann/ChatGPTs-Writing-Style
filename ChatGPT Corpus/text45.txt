### Abstract

This study investigates the effects of simultaneous root colonization by three species of the arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) Glomus on soil pore characteristics. AMF play a crucial role in soil structure and function through their symbiotic relationships with plant roots. By colonizing roots, these fungi can influence soil aggregation, pore formation, and overall soil health. This research aims to elucidate how co-colonization by Glomus species affects soil pore characteristics, which are critical for soil aeration, water infiltration, and root growth. Using a combination of field experiments and laboratory analyses, we examined soil samples from plots colonized by Glomus intraradices, Glomus etunicatum, and Glomus mosseae, both individually and in combination. Our findings indicate that simultaneous colonization by multiple Glomus species significantly alters soil pore structure compared to single-species colonization and non-colonized controls. Notably, co-colonization led to increased pore connectivity and enhanced soil aggregation, which can positively impact soil aeration and water movement. These results highlight the complex interactions between mycorrhizal fungi and soil properties, offering insights into the potential benefits of using multiple AMF species for improving soil health and fertility in agricultural systems.

### Introduction

#### Background

Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) form symbiotic relationships with the roots of most terrestrial plants, facilitating nutrient uptake, especially phosphorus, and improving soil structure. These fungi produce extensive networks of hyphae that extend into the soil, interacting with soil particles and influencing soil properties. One of the key aspects of soil health and functionality is the formation and maintenance of soil pores, which are essential for soil aeration, water infiltration, and root growth.

Soil pore characteristics are influenced by various factors, including soil type, plant root systems, and microbial activity. AMF contribute to soil pore formation through several mechanisms, such as hyphal binding of soil particles, the production of glomalin (a glycoprotein that helps stabilize soil aggregates), and the creation of macropores that improve soil structure. However, most studies have focused on the effects of single AMF species on soil properties. The effects of simultaneous colonization by multiple AMF species, especially on soil pore characteristics, remain underexplored.

#### Motivation

Understanding how simultaneous root colonization by multiple AMF species impacts soil pore characteristics is crucial for several reasons:

1. **Soil Structure Improvement**: Enhanced soil structure can lead to better soil aeration, water infiltration, and root growth, which are vital for plant health and agricultural productivity.
2. **Agricultural Practices**: Insights into the synergistic effects of multiple AMF species can inform agricultural practices aimed at improving soil health and fertility through the use of mycorrhizal inoculants.
3. **Ecological Understanding**: Studying the interactions between different AMF species and soil properties contributes to our understanding of soil ecology and the role of mycorrhizal fungi in natural and managed ecosystems.

#### Objectives and Scope

The primary objectives of this study are to:

1. **Assess Soil Pore Characteristics**: Evaluate the effects of simultaneous root colonization by three Glomus species—Glomus intraradices, Glomus etunicatum, and Glomus mosseae—on soil pore characteristics.
2. **Compare Single vs. Multiple Species Colonization**: Compare soil pore characteristics in plots colonized by single Glomus species versus those with simultaneous colonization by multiple species and non-colonized controls.
3. **Understand Mechanisms**: Explore the mechanisms through which co-colonization by multiple AMF species influences soil pore structure and soil aggregation.

The scope of the study includes field experiments to assess soil pore characteristics and laboratory analyses to quantify and compare the effects of AMF colonization.

#### Methodology

1. **Field Experiments**: Establish experimental plots with plants inoculated with single AMF species (Glomus intraradices, Glomus etunicatum, or Glomus mosseae) and plots inoculated with a combination of all three species. Include control plots without AMF inoculation.
2. **Soil Sampling and Analysis**: Collect soil samples from the experimental plots and analyze soil pore characteristics using techniques such as soil porosity measurements, image analysis of soil pore structure, and soil aggregate stability tests.
3. **Data Interpretation**: Compare soil pore characteristics across different treatments to assess the impact of simultaneous AMF colonization on soil pore formation and stability.

#### Literature Review

Previous research on the effects of AMF on soil properties has provided valuable insights:

- **Jastrow et al. (1998)**: Investigated the role of AMF in soil aggregation and found that AMF hyphae contribute to soil aggregate stability through the production of glomalin.
- **Rillig (2004)**: Reviewed the influence of AMF on soil structure and highlighted the role of hyphal networks in improving soil pore connectivity and aggregation.
- **Smith and Read (2008)**: Examined the effects of different AMF species on plant growth and soil health, noting variations in soil properties depending on the AMF species and their interactions with plant roots.

These studies have established the benefits of AMF for soil structure but have generally focused on single-species effects rather than the interactions between multiple AMF species.

3. **Glomus Species**: A genus of AMF known for its role in soil aggregation and nutrient uptake. The species studied are Glomus intraradices, Glomus etunicatum, and Glomus mosseae.
